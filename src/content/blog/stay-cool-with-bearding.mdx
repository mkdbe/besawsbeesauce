---
title: Stay Cool with Bearding
date: "2018-07-01"
excerpt: It's 90°F for the third day in a row, and the bees have a fascinating solution to the heat — they hang out on the outside of the hive in a living, breathing beard.
---

It's over 90°F outside for the third day in a row. You'd think that because honeybees keep their hive at an average of 95°F year-round, the weather would be no big deal.

Imagine having a small, nicely air-conditioned house on a day like today. You decide to invite 100 people over for a party. The house no longer feels pleasant — it's overcrowded, stuffy, and the central air is struggling. What would you do?

For bees, this is where **bearding** comes in.

At this time of year, a hive can be approaching its peak population — up to 80,000 bees. In order to regulate the heat inside and keep the queen at her preferred temperature, many of the bees form a thick cluster on the front of the hive. It looks like a beard, hanging off the bottom board and front face of the box.

The amount of bearding can tell you a lot about how a hive is doing. A strong hive in hot weather will have a dense, full beard. A weak hive may show almost nothing — not because they're comfortable, but because there aren't enough bees.

With heat like this, it's also a good reminder: **put out water for your bees.** They're thirsty too.

**Interesting fact:** Some bees are specifically tasked with collecting water to bring back to the hive. They spread it across the honeycomb and fan their wings to move cool air through — evaporative cooling, bee style.
