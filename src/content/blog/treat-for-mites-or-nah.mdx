---
title: Treat for Mites, or Nah?
date: "2018-05-27"
excerpt: The most divisive question in backyard beekeeping — to treat for varroa mites or leave the bees alone. Here's where we landed, and what it cost us to get there.
---

This is a hot topic in the beekeeping community, and no opinion is necessarily right. Varroa mites are currently the number one killer of honeybee colonies. They're parasitic bugs that weaken bees, damage wings, and kill colonies off relatively quickly.

The mites lay eggs on bee larvae and hatch around the same time the new bee emerges. They attach and feed on the bees continuously, causing:

1. Weakened bees and larvae
2. Shorter lifespans
3. Reduced ability to navigate and find their way home
4. Less pollen and nectar brought back to the hive
5. Bees born with deformed wings that can't fly

So — obviously treat, right? That's actually very debatable.

For many beekeepers, using chemicals to control mite populations feels wrong. Bees have existed for millions of years without human intervention. The counterargument: leave them alone.

I wanted to avoid treatment too. Here's what that cost us:

- **Year 1:** No treatment. All hives died.
- **Year 2:** Treated 1 out of 2 hives as an experiment. The untreated hive died in early fall.
- **Year 3:** Planned to wait until fall. One hive died by early September.
- **Year 4:** So many swarms we didn't know when to treat. Our strongest, most productive hive died in September.

This year we treated both surviving hives with **Mite-Away Quick-Strips** (formic acid). Treatment can be done in 7 days before honey supers go on, which fits our timing. The bees were very unhappy about the strong scent — they bearded heavily on the front of the hive for the first 24 hours.

Hopefully someday bees will develop a natural tolerance to varroa. Until then, we treat as needed.

**Interesting fact:** Varroa mites have a particular preference for nurse bees.
